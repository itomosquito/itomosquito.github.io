Creating Accurate, Contextually Sensitive, Computational Models of Human Decision Making

My research interests revolve around creating accurate computational models of the human decision process. Creating these models is a vital step towards the realization of socially intelligent systems capable of predicting, simulating, and analyzing human behavior. This is critical across a diverse range of applications such as those found in social simulation, virtual-human based training, interactive health intervention, embodied conversational agents, and decision support systems.

One significant challenge in creating accurate models of human behavior is accounting for the effect of context on decision behavior. Research has shown that seemingly minor changes to the context in which a scenario is presented, or framed, can lead to drastic shifts in behavior, a phenomenon collectively referred to as framing effects. In a seminal study by Amos Tversky and Daniel Kahneman, now referred to as the Asian Disease Study, Tversky and Kahneman showed that human decision behavior could be drastically altered simply by changing the manner in which outcomes are described. Given the complexities in both computationally representing context, an inherently ill-defined concept in itself, and subsequently modeling its effect on the decision process most computational approaches to decision making generally do not address framing effects. Therefore, my thesis work has primarily focused on creating and empirically validating a computational decision framework which explicitly provides principled guidance for the identification and encoding of context as well as a sound decision-theoretic approach for modeling its effects on pre-existing preferences.

My approach for integrating considerations of context into the computational decision process hinges on integrating the psychological theory of appraisal with decision-theoretic approaches to decision making. Appraisal Theory addresses the process by which emotions arise from an individual's appraisal of their relationship with the environment along several discrete dimensions such as control, goal conduciveness, and pleasantness. In particular, Appraisal Theory asserts that various combinations of these appraisals comprise or give rise to different emotions, e.g., happy, sad, angry. Furthermore, a great deal of existing research in diverse fields such as economics, psychology, cognitive science, and operation science, has established the significant influence that emotions can have on behavior. However, no computational models had previously exploited the principles of appraisal theory in order to explicitly address the influence of context in the decision process. Therefore, the key insight in my work is to construe appraisals, essentially discrete contextual evaluations of the environment, as ``contextual sensors'' and integrate them directly into the computational decision process. This allows for the integration of general, flexible, and extensible notions of context into a computational model of decision making in a highly principled, effective manner, and straightforward manner.
